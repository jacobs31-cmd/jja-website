Do You Need Business Insurance as an LLC or Sole Proprietor in Michigan?

By Joseph Jacobs·June 29, 2026·7 min read

One of the most common — and most expensive — misconceptions we hear from new Michigan business owners is "I formed an LLC, so I don't really need insurance." An LLC is a valuable legal structure, but it does a very different job than an insurance policy, and confusing the two leaves a lot of owners exposed. Here's what an LLC actually protects, what it doesn't, and the coverage that fills the gap.

What an LLC does — and doesn't — do

A limited liability company creates a legal separation between your business and your personal assets. If the business is sued or owes a debt, that separation can keep creditors from reaching your house, your car, and your personal savings. That's real protection, and it's worth having.

But notice what it does not do:

  • It doesn't pay claims. If a customer is injured or you damage someone's property, the LLC shields your personal assets — but the business still has to pay the judgment or settlement, which can wipe out the company you built.
  • It doesn't cover your own property. An LLC offers zero protection for your tools, inventory, equipment, or building if there's a fire, theft, or storm.
  • It doesn't protect you from your own negligence. Courts can hold an owner personally responsible for their own professional mistakes or actions, LLC or not.
  • The shield can be "pierced." If you commingle personal and business finances or don't maintain the LLC properly, a court can set the protection aside and reach your personal assets anyway.

In short: an LLC limits who can be reached in a lawsuit. Insurance pays the actual bill. You want both.

Sole proprietors: even more exposed

If you haven't formed an LLC or corporation at all, you're a sole proprietor by default — and there is no legal separation between you and your business. A lawsuit or business debt can come straight for your personal assets. For a sole proprietor, business insurance isn't just protecting the company; it's protecting your home and savings directly. Many Michigan sole proprietors are one accident away from a personal financial crisis without realizing it.

Coverages that actually matter for small businesses

  • General Liability (GL). The foundation. Covers third-party bodily injury and property damage — the customer who slips, the equipment you accidentally damage on a job. Most clients, landlords, and contracts require it.
  • Business Owner's Policy (BOP). Bundles general liability with property coverage for your equipment, inventory, and space — usually the most cost-effective package for a small business.
  • Professional Liability (E&O). If you give advice or provide a professional service, this covers claims that your work caused a financial loss. GL won't.
  • Commercial Auto. If you or employees drive for the business, your personal auto policy likely excludes business use. (We cover this in detail in our commercial auto article.)
  • Workers' Compensation. In Michigan, workers' comp is generally required once you have employees — the thresholds are specific, and the penalties for going without are steep.

Why clients and landlords will ask anyway

Even if you're comfortable with the risk, the market often isn't. Commercial landlords, general contractors, and larger customers routinely require a certificate of insurance — proof of general liability, sometimes with specific limits — before they'll sign a lease, let you on a job site, or hire you. Without coverage, you simply can't take the work. An LLC certificate won't satisfy that requirement; a liability policy will.

Frequently Asked Questions

If I have an LLC, do I still need business insurance?

Yes. An LLC limits which assets a lawsuit can reach, but it doesn't pay claims or cover your own equipment and property — insurance does. The two work together.

Does a sole proprietor need business insurance?

Even more so. A sole proprietor has no legal separation between business and personal assets, so a claim or judgment can reach your home and savings directly.

What coverage does a small Michigan business usually start with?

General liability — often bundled into a Business Owner's Policy with property coverage — plus workers' compensation once you have employees, and professional liability if you give advice or provide a service.

The bottom line

An LLC and business insurance solve two different problems: the LLC limits which assets are exposed, and insurance pays the claim so it never gets that far. Relying on the LLC alone leaves your business — and, if you're a sole proprietor, your personal finances — open to the very losses you formed the company to avoid. The good news is that coverage for a small Michigan business is usually more affordable than owners expect.
