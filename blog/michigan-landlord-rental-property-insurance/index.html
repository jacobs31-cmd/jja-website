Michigan Landlord & Rental Property Insurance: What You Need

By Joseph Jacobs·June 29, 2026·7 min read

If you rent out a house, condo, or duplex in Michigan, here's the mistake that costs landlords the most: assuming a homeowners policy covers a property you don't live in. It doesn't. The moment a property becomes a rental, you need a landlord policy (sometimes called a dwelling or rental property policy). Here's what it covers, what it costs, and the gaps that surprise first-time landlords.

Why your homeowners policy won't cover a rental

A homeowners policy is written for an owner-occupied home. Once tenants move in and you move out, the risk profile changes completely — different liability exposure, no owner on-site, and a building used to generate income. Insurers exclude that use from a standard homeowners policy, so a claim on a rented-out home can be denied outright. A landlord policy is built for exactly this situation.

What a Michigan landlord policy covers

  • The dwelling and other structures. The building itself, plus detached structures like a garage or shed, against fire, wind, and other covered perils.
  • Landlord liability. If a tenant or visitor is injured on the property and you're found responsible, this covers defense costs and damages.
  • Fair rental value / loss of rents. If a covered loss makes the unit uninhabitable, this reimburses the rental income you lose while it's being repaired — often the most overlooked and valuable piece.
  • Landlord-owned contents. Appliances, furnishings, and equipment you provide (a furnished rental, lawn equipment, the washer and dryer) can be covered.

What it does NOT cover

  • Your tenant's belongings. Their furniture and possessions are their responsibility — which is why you should require renters insurance in the lease.
  • Flood. Like a homeowners policy, a landlord policy excludes flood damage. If the property is anywhere near water or a flood zone, you need separate flood insurance.
  • Short-term / Airbnb use. A standard landlord policy assumes a long-term lease. Frequent short-term rentals are treated more like a business and usually need a specific short-term rental endorsement or policy.
  • Wear, neglect, and vacancy. Ordinary maintenance isn't covered, and most policies limit coverage once a unit sits vacant beyond a set number of days.

One unit or several? It changes the policy

A single rental home or a small 2-4 unit building is usually written on a dwelling/landlord policy. Once you own larger multi-unit buildings or a growing portfolio, it often makes more sense to move to a commercial policy that can bundle multiple properties and higher liability limits. If you're scaling up, tell us — the right structure saves money and closes gaps.

What landlord insurance costs in Michigan

As a rule of thumb, a landlord policy runs somewhat more than a comparable homeowners policy on the same building — often in the range of 15% to 25% higher — because of the added liability and income-protection coverage. The exact premium depends on the property's age, location, condition, coverage limits, and whether it's a single-family rental or a multi-unit. Requiring tenant renters insurance and keeping the property well-maintained both help hold the cost down.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my homeowners policy cover a property I rent out?

No. Once a home is rented to tenants rather than owner-occupied, a standard homeowners policy typically excludes it, and a claim can be denied. You need a landlord or dwelling policy.

Do my tenants need their own insurance?

Yes — your landlord policy covers the building and your liability, not the tenant's belongings. You can and should require renters insurance in the lease; it's inexpensive and protects everyone.

Does landlord insurance cover Airbnb or short-term rentals?

Usually not under a standard landlord policy, which assumes a long-term lease. Regular short-term rental activity is treated more like a business and needs a short-term rental endorsement or a separate policy.

The bottom line

If you're collecting rent on a Michigan property, a landlord policy isn't optional — it's the difference between a covered claim and a denied one. The single most important step is making sure the property is insured for its actual use, not as if you still lived there, and that you're not missing loss-of-rents and liability coverage.
