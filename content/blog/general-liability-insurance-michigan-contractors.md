<h2>What General Liability Insurance Covers</h2>

<p>General liability (GL) insurance protects your business from third-party claims — meaning claims from people outside your company who are injured or whose property is damaged as a result of your work. The three main coverage areas:</p>

<h3>Bodily injury</h3>
<p>A customer or bystander is injured at a jobsite where you&#39;re working. They sue. GL covers your legal defense and any settlement or judgment — up to your policy limits. This includes injuries that happen during the job and, in many cases, after the job is completed.</p>

<h3>Property damage</h3>
<p>You or your crew accidentally damages a client&#39;s property while working — a tile contractor cracks a countertop, an HVAC tech puts a foot through a ceiling, an excavator clips a gas line. GL covers the repair or replacement cost and your legal exposure if the client pursues damages.</p>

<h3>Completed operations</h3>
<p>Your work is finished and the client has signed off — but a month later, something you installed or built causes a problem. Completed operations coverage extends your GL protection to claims that arise after the job is done. For contractors, this is one of the most important components of the policy — construction defect claims often surface months or years after project completion.</p>

<h2>What GL Does Not Cover</h2>

<p>General liability is essential, but it has clear limits. Michigan contractors often discover these gaps at the worst possible moment — after a claim is filed:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Your own employees&#39; injuries.</strong> That&#39;s workers&#39; compensation. In Michigan, if you have three or more employees (or one working 35+ hours/week for 13+ consecutive weeks), workers&#39; comp is legally required.</li>
  <li><strong>Your tools and equipment.</strong> GL doesn&#39;t cover your own property. You need an inland marine or tools-and-equipment policy for that.</li>
  <li><strong>Your work vehicles.</strong> Commercial auto is a separate policy. Personal auto policies typically exclude business use.</li>
  <li><strong>Professional errors or faulty design.</strong> If you&#39;re providing design services or professional advice, that&#39;s professional liability (E&amp;O) territory.</li>
  <li><strong>Intentional acts or contract disputes.</strong> GL is for accidents, not disputes over payment, workmanship warranties, or intentional misconduct.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Additional Coverages Most Michigan Contractors Need</h2>

<p>GL is the foundation, but a complete contractor insurance program typically includes:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Workers&#39; compensation</strong> — required in Michigan for most operations with employees; also protects sole proprietors if they elect to include themselves</li>
  <li><strong>Commercial auto</strong> — if you drive vehicles for work, personal auto won&#39;t cover a business-use accident</li>
  <li><strong>Tools and equipment / inland marine</strong> — covers your gear on and off the jobsite</li>
  <li><strong>Builder&#39;s risk</strong> — covers structures under construction against fire, theft, and weather before completion</li>
  <li><strong>Commercial umbrella</strong> — extends your GL and auto limits for larger exposures; increasingly required by commercial clients and municipalities</li>
</ul>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Do I need general liability insurance if I&#39;m a solo contractor with no employees?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Yes. Your liability exposure has nothing to do with whether you have employees — it has to do with the work you perform and what could go wrong. A solo electrician can cause just as much property damage or injury as a 10-person crew. Most permit offices, general contractors, and homeowners won&#39;t let you on the job without a current GL certificate regardless of your business size.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>What is a certificate of insurance and why does everyone ask for one?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>A certificate of insurance (COI) is a one-page document from your insurer that summarizes your active coverage — policy type, limits, carrier, and effective dates. General contractors, property owners, and municipalities request them before allowing you to work on their projects. It&#39;s proof that you have coverage and that they won&#39;t be left exposed if something goes wrong. Your insurer or agent can generate one quickly when you need it — usually within a day.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>What does "additional insured" mean on a contractor policy?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>When a general contractor or property owner asks to be listed as an "additional insured" on your policy, they&#39;re asking for protection under your GL coverage if they&#39;re named in a lawsuit arising from your work. It&#39;s a standard request on commercial jobsites and in most subcontracts. Your insurer adds them via endorsement — your agent handles this when you request a certificate for that specific job.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>My general contractor said I need $2 million in coverage. How does that work?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>The $2 million figure typically refers to your aggregate limit — the maximum your policy pays across all claims in a policy year. Your per-occurrence limit (the most paid for any single claim) is usually $1 million. A $1M/$2M policy structure is the standard requirement for most Michigan commercial subcontract work. If your current policy has lower limits, we can adjust them — often for a modest premium increase.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Does GL cover damage to the work itself, or just damage I cause to other things?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Standard GL excludes damage to your own work — the specific work product you delivered. It covers damage you cause to other people&#39;s property while doing your job. For example: an HVAC contractor accidentally cracks a homeowner&#39;s hardwood floor — that&#39;s covered. But if the HVAC unit they installed fails and needs to be replaced — that&#39;s a workmanship issue, not a GL claim. Completed operations covers injury or property damage caused by your completed work, but not repair or replacement of the work itself.</p>
  </div>
</details>
