How Much Does Business Insurance Cost in Michigan?

By Joseph Jacobs·June 12, 2026·7 min read

"What's this going to cost me?" is the first question almost every Michigan business owner asks — and the honest answer is "it depends." But it depends on a handful of specific, knowable things. Here's a realistic look at what business insurance actually costs in Michigan in 2026, broken down by coverage and industry, plus the levers that move your premium up or down.

Typical Michigan business insurance costs by coverage

These are ballpark annual ranges for a small-to-midsize Michigan business. Your actual numbers depend on the factors below, but this gives you a feel for the order of magnitude:

  • General liability — roughly $400 to $1,500 a year for many small businesses; higher-risk trades pay more. It's the foundation policy most contracts and landlords require.
  • Business Owner's Policy (BOP) — often $500 to $3,000 a year. A BOP bundles general liability and commercial property, usually for 10–20% less than buying them separately.
  • Workers' compensation — priced as a rate per $100 of payroll, varying widely by job classification. An office at well under $1 per $100 of payroll; a roofing or construction class many times that.
  • Commercial auto — commonly $1,500 to $2,500+ per vehicle per year in Michigan, influenced heavily by our No-Fault system and the vehicle's use.
  • Professional liability (E&O) — often a few hundred to a couple thousand dollars for a solo professional, scaling with revenue and risk.
  • Cyber liability — frequently under a few thousand dollars for a small business, and far cheaper than the average breach it protects against.

What drives your premium

Two businesses on the same street can pay very different premiums. The biggest factors:

  • Industry and class code. Risk is everything. A consulting firm, a restaurant, and a roofing contractor are priced on completely different scales.
  • Revenue and payroll. Most commercial policies are rated on sales or payroll, so larger operations pay more.
  • Claims history. Past claims — and for workers' comp, your experience modifier (Mod) — directly raise or lower your rate, sometimes for years.
  • Coverage limits and deductibles. Higher limits and lower deductibles cost more. The right balance depends on your contracts and risk tolerance.
  • Location and property. Building age, construction, and where you operate affect property and liability pricing.

How Michigan business owners pay less without cutting coverage

Cheaper isn't the goal — the right coverage at the best available price is. A few proven ways to get there:

  • Bundle into a BOP where you're eligible — it's typically 10–20% cheaper than monoline policies.
  • Get your workers' comp classifications right. Misclassified payroll is the single most common reason Michigan businesses overpay.
  • Manage your claims and Mod. A clean loss history compounds into lower premiums year after year.
  • Shop the whole market. Commercial pricing swings dramatically between carriers for the same business. One quote tells you almost nothing.

Why an independent agent matters for the price

A captive agent can show you one company's rate. The bottom line

There's no flat answer to "how much does business insurance cost in Michigan," but the ranges above plus your industry, revenue, payroll, and claims history get you close.
