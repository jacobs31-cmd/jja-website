<p class="lead">"What's this going to cost me?" is the first question almost every Michigan business owner asks — and the honest answer is "it depends." But it depends on a handful of specific, knowable things. Here's a realistic look at what business insurance actually costs in Michigan in 2026, broken down by coverage and industry, plus the levers that move your premium up or down.</p>

<h2>Typical Michigan business insurance costs by coverage</h2>
<p>These are ballpark annual ranges for a small-to-midsize Michigan business. Your actual numbers depend on the factors below, but this gives you a feel for the order of magnitude:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="../../business/general-liability-insurance/">General liability</a></strong> — roughly $400 to $1,500 a year for many small businesses; higher-risk trades pay more. It's the foundation policy most contracts and landlords require.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="../../business/business-owners-policy/">Business Owner's Policy (BOP)</a></strong> — often $500 to $3,000 a year. A BOP bundles general liability and commercial property, usually for 10–20% less than buying them separately.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="../../business/workers-compensation/">Workers' compensation</a></strong> — priced as a rate per $100 of payroll, varying widely by job classification. An office at well under $1 per $100 of payroll; a roofing or construction class many times that.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="../../business/commercial-auto-insurance/">Commercial auto</a></strong> — commonly $1,500 to $2,500+ per vehicle per year in Michigan, influenced heavily by our No-Fault system and the vehicle's use.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="../../business/professional-liability-insurance/">Professional liability (E&amp;O)</a></strong> — often a few hundred to a couple thousand dollars for a solo professional, scaling with revenue and risk.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="../../business/cyber-liability-insurance/">Cyber liability</a></strong> — frequently under a few thousand dollars for a small business, and far cheaper than the average breach it protects against.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What drives your premium</h2>
<p>Two businesses on the same street can pay very different premiums. The biggest factors:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Industry and class code.</strong> Risk is everything. A consulting firm, a restaurant, and a roofing contractor are priced on completely different scales.</li>
  <li><strong>Revenue and payroll.</strong> Most commercial policies are rated on sales or payroll, so larger operations pay more.</li>
  <li><strong>Claims history.</strong> Past claims — and for workers' comp, your experience modifier (Mod) — directly raise or lower your rate, sometimes for years.</li>
  <li><strong>Coverage limits and deductibles.</strong> Higher limits and lower deductibles cost more. The right balance depends on your contracts and risk tolerance.</li>
  <li><strong>Location and property.</strong> Building age, construction, and where you operate affect property and liability pricing.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How Michigan business owners pay less without cutting coverage</h2>
<p>Cheaper isn't the goal — the right coverage at the best available price is. A few proven ways to get there:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Bundle into a BOP</strong> where you're eligible — it's typically 10–20% cheaper than monoline policies.</li>
  <li><strong>Get your workers' comp classifications right.</strong> Misclassified payroll is the single most common reason Michigan businesses overpay.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage your claims and Mod.</strong> A clean loss history compounds into lower premiums year after year.</li>
  <li><strong>Shop the whole market.</strong> Commercial pricing swings dramatically between carriers for the same business. One quote tells you almost nothing.</li>
</ul>
