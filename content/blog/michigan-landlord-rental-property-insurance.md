<p class="lead">If you rent out a house, condo, or duplex in Michigan, here's the mistake that costs landlords the most: assuming a homeowners policy covers a property you don't live in. It doesn't. The moment a property becomes a rental, you need a <strong>landlord policy</strong> (sometimes called a dwelling or rental property policy). Here's what it covers, what it costs, and the gaps that surprise first-time landlords.</p>

<h2>Why your homeowners policy won't cover a rental</h2>
<p>A homeowners policy is written for an owner-occupied home. Once tenants move in and you move out, the risk profile changes completely — different liability exposure, no owner on-site, and a building used to generate income. Insurers exclude that use from a standard <a href="../../personal/home-insurance/">homeowners policy</a>, so a claim on a rented-out home can be denied outright. A landlord policy is built for exactly this situation.</p>

<h2>What a Michigan landlord policy covers</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>The dwelling and other structures.</strong> The building itself, plus detached structures like a garage or shed, against fire, wind, and other covered perils.</li>
  <li><strong>Landlord liability.</strong> If a tenant or visitor is injured on the property and you're found responsible, this covers defense costs and damages.</li>
  <li><strong>Fair rental value / loss of rents.</strong> If a covered loss makes the unit uninhabitable, this reimburses the rental income you lose while it's being repaired — often the most overlooked and valuable piece.</li>
  <li><strong>Landlord-owned contents.</strong> Appliances, furnishings, and equipment you provide (a furnished rental, lawn equipment, the washer and dryer) can be covered.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What it does NOT cover</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Your tenant's belongings.</strong> Their furniture and possessions are their responsibility — which is why you should require <a href="../../blog/michigan-renters-insurance/">renters insurance</a> in the lease.</li>
  <li><strong>Flood.</strong> Like a homeowners policy, a landlord policy excludes flood damage. If the property is anywhere near water or a flood zone, you need separate <a href="../../blog/michigan-flood-insurance/">flood insurance</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Short-term / Airbnb use.</strong> A standard landlord policy assumes a long-term lease. Frequent short-term rentals are treated more like a business and usually need a specific short-term rental endorsement or policy.</li>
  <li><strong>Wear, neglect, and vacancy.</strong> Ordinary maintenance isn't covered, and most policies limit coverage once a unit sits vacant beyond a set number of days.</li>
</ul>

<h2>One unit or several? It changes the policy</h2>
<p>A single rental home or a small 2-4 unit building is usually written on a dwelling/landlord policy. Once you own larger multi-unit buildings or a growing portfolio, it often makes more sense to move to a <a href="../../business/">commercial</a> policy that can bundle multiple properties and higher liability limits. If you're scaling up, tell us — the right structure saves money and closes gaps.</p>

<h2>What landlord insurance costs in Michigan</h2>
<p>As a rule of thumb, a landlord policy runs somewhat more than a comparable homeowners policy on the same building — often in the range of 15% to 25% higher — because of the added liability and income-protection coverage. The exact premium depends on the property's age, location, condition, coverage limits, and whether it's a single-family rental or a multi-unit. Requiring tenant renters insurance and keeping the property well-maintained both help hold the cost down.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>Does my homeowners policy cover a property I rent out?</h3>
<p>No. Once a home is rented to tenants rather than owner-occupied, a standard homeowners policy typically excludes it, and a claim can be denied. You need a landlord or dwelling policy.</p>
<h3>Do my tenants need their own insurance?</h3>
<p>Yes — your landlord policy covers the building and your liability, not the tenant's belongings. You can and should require renters insurance in the lease; it's inexpensive and protects everyone.</p>
<h3>Does landlord insurance cover Airbnb or short-term rentals?</h3>
<p>Usually not under a standard landlord policy, which assumes a long-term lease. Regular short-term rental activity is treated more like a business and needs a short-term rental endorsement or a separate policy.</p>
