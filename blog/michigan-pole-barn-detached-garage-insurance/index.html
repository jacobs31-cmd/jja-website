Does Michigan Home Insurance Cover a Pole Barn or Detached Garage?

By Joseph Jacobs·June 29, 2026·7 min read

Drive ten minutes outside almost any Michigan town and you'll see them — pole barns, detached garages, and outbuildings holding everything from a tractor to a boat to a side business. Most owners assume their homeowners policy covers these structures automatically. It often does, but with limits and exclusions that catch people off guard after a fire or a windstorm. Here's exactly how the coverage works and where it tends to fall short.

Where a pole barn fits in your policy

A standard Michigan homeowners policy splits coverage into parts. Your house is covered under Coverage A (Dwelling). Structures that are not attached to the house — a detached garage, a pole barn, a shed, a fence, a gazebo — fall under Coverage B, "Other Structures."

The catch is how Coverage B is sized. On most policies it defaults to 10% of your dwelling limit. So if your home is insured for $400,000, you have roughly $40,000 to rebuild all of your detached structures combined. For a small shed that's plenty. For a large, finished pole barn with concrete, electrical, and plumbing, $40,000 may not come close to the rebuild cost.

The gaps that surprise people

  • The limit is too low. A big pole barn can cost more to rebuild than the default 10% allows. After a total loss you'd cover the shortfall out of pocket.
  • Business use voids the coverage. This is the biggest one. If you run a business out of the barn — woodworking, auto repair, landscaping storage, a side operation — a homeowners policy typically excludes structures used for business. That use needs a commercial or farm policy.
  • Farming use is excluded too. If the barn stores commercial farm equipment or supports an active farm, a standard homeowners form usually won't respond. A farmowners policy is the right tool.
  • What's inside is rated differently. Coverage B insures the structure itself. The contents — tools, ATVs, snowblowers, furniture — fall under your homeowners personal property coverage, which has its own limits and special caps on certain items.
  • Vehicles and "toys" aren't covered as contents. A car, boat, motorcycle, ATV, snowmobile, or RV stored in the barn is not covered by your home policy if it's damaged or stolen — those need their own auto or recreational-vehicle policies.

Pole barns built for living or hobbies

"Barndominiums" and finished pole barns with living space, heat, and plumbing are increasingly common in Michigan. These blur the line between an outbuilding and a dwelling, and they often need to be specifically described and valued on the policy rather than left to the default Coverage B percentage. If you've finished off part of a barn, tell your agent — an undisclosed finished space is a claim waiting to be underpaid or denied.

How to make sure your barn is actually protected

  1. Find your Coverage B limit. It's on your declarations page, usually listed as "Other Structures." Compare it to what your barn and other detached structures would actually cost to rebuild today.
  2. Increase the limit if needed. Carriers will raise Coverage B above the default 10% for a modest premium, or schedule a specific structure for its full value.
  3. Be honest about how it's used. Storage and personal hobbies are fine on a homeowners policy. Any business or farming use needs the right commercial or farm coverage layered in.
  4. Insure the contents and the toys separately. Make sure valuable tools and equipment fit within your personal property limit, and that vehicles stored inside carry their own policies.
  5. Document it. Photos, dimensions, and build details make a claim go smoothly and help set the right limit up front.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my homeowners policy automatically cover a detached pole barn?

Usually yes, under Coverage B (Other Structures) — but only up to a default limit, often about 10% of your dwelling coverage, and only for personal (non-business) use.

Is a pole barn used for a business covered by home insurance?

No. Business use — and farming use — is typically excluded on a homeowners policy and needs a commercial or farmowners policy instead.

Are the vehicles and tools stored inside the barn covered?

Tools and equipment fall under your personal property limit, but vehicles like ATVs, boats, snowmobiles, and tractors are not covered as home contents — they need their own policies.

The bottom line

Your Michigan homeowners policy probably does cover a detached garage or pole barn — but only up to a default limit, only for personal use, and only the structure itself, not the vehicles parked inside. The single most important step is checking your "Other Structures" limit against the real rebuild cost and telling your agent how the barn is used. Send us your declarations page and a quick description of your outbuildings, and we'll confirm whether you're properly covered or one storm away from a nasty surprise.
