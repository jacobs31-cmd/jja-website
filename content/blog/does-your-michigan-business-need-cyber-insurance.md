<h2>What Your Current Business Insurance Does NOT Cover</h2>

<p>This is the part that surprises most business owners. Your General Liability policy, your BOP (Business Owner's Policy), and your commercial property coverage were all written before cyber threats existed at this scale. They have gaps — big ones.</p>

<h3>General Liability</h3>
<p>Standard GL covers bodily injury and property damage to third parties. It does not cover the cost of a data breach, ransomware recovery, business interruption from a hack, or notifying customers that their data was exposed. Some GL policies have been expanded with limited cyber endorsements, but the coverage is usually minimal.</p>

<h3>Business Owner's Policy (BOP)</h3>
<p>A BOP bundles GL and commercial property. Same story — it covers your physical assets and basic liability, but "electronic data" is typically excluded or heavily sublimited. If ransomware encrypts your entire server, a standard BOP won't pay to recover it.</p>

<h3>Commercial Property</h3>
<p>Covers physical damage to your building and equipment. A server wiped by a cyberattack isn't "physical damage" in the traditional insurance sense. Most commercial property policies explicitly exclude losses caused by cyber events.</p>

<div class="callout-box">
<p><strong>Michigan businesses take note:</strong> Michigan's data breach notification law (MCL 445.72) requires businesses to notify affected customers — and potentially the state attorney general — when a breach occurs. The legal and administrative cost of compliance alone can run tens of thousands of dollars. Cyber insurance covers this. Your GL policy does not.</p>
</div>

<h2>What Cyber Insurance Actually Covers</h2>

<p>A standalone cyber liability policy has two main components — first-party and third-party coverage.</p>

<h3>First-Party Coverage (Your Own Losses)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Business interruption:</strong> Lost revenue while your systems are down after an attack</li>
  <li><strong>Data recovery:</strong> Cost to restore or rebuild data and systems after a breach or ransomware</li>
  <li><strong>Ransomware payments:</strong> The ransom itself, if paying is the most viable option (carrier involvement required)</li>
  <li><strong>Cyber extortion response:</strong> Negotiation services and forensics</li>
  <li><strong>Notification costs:</strong> Legally required customer notifications, credit monitoring for affected individuals</li>
  <li><strong>Crisis management:</strong> PR support to manage reputational damage after a public breach</li>
</ul>

<h3>Third-Party Coverage (Claims Against You)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Data privacy liability:</strong> If customer or employee data is exposed and they sue you</li>
  <li><strong>Regulatory fines and penalties:</strong> Government fines related to a breach or data mishandling</li>
  <li><strong>Media liability:</strong> Claims related to content you publish electronically</li>
  <li><strong>Network security liability:</strong> If your system infects a third party or enables an attack on someone else</li>
</ul>

<h2>Which Michigan Businesses Need It Most</h2>

<p>Almost every business that uses a computer or stores any customer information should be thinking about this. But the risk is particularly high for certain types of businesses:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Retailers and restaurants</strong> that process credit and debit card payments — PCI compliance issues + customer card data exposure</li>
  <li><strong>Healthcare and dental offices</strong> — HIPAA violations from a breach can trigger massive regulatory penalties</li>
  <li><strong>Professional services</strong> (accountants, attorneys, consultants) — you hold sensitive client financial and personal data</li>
  <li><strong>Contractors and trades</strong> that use project management software, store client contracts, or invoice electronically</li>
  <li><strong>Any business with employees</strong> — employee HR records, payroll data, and direct deposit information are high-value targets</li>
  <li><strong>Businesses with a website that collects any data</strong> — even a basic contact form creates exposure</li>
</ul>

<p>The short version: if you use email, store customer information, or process payments — any of those things — you have cyber exposure. The question isn't whether you could be targeted. It's whether you can afford to absorb the hit without insurance.</p>

<h2>What Insurers Are Requiring Now</h2>

<p>Getting cyber insurance in 2026 isn't as simple as filling out an application. Carriers have tightened underwriting significantly after the ransomware wave of the early 2020s. Most now require basic security controls before they'll issue a policy:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Multi-factor authentication (MFA) on email, remote access, and admin accounts — 96% of cyber insurers now mandate this</li>
  <li>Regular data backups (ideally offsite or cloud-based)</li>
  <li>Endpoint protection (antivirus/EDR) on all company devices</li>
  <li>Employee security awareness training</li>
  <li>Patch management — keeping software and systems updated</li>
</ul>

<p>If you don't have these in place, some carriers will decline coverage entirely. Others will cover you at a higher premium or with exclusions. This is worth knowing before you apply — and it's one more reason to work with an agent who shops multiple carriers rather than going direct to one company.</p>

<h2>How Much Does It Cost?</h2>

<p>For most Michigan small businesses, cyber liability insurance runs <strong>$500 to $3,000 per year</strong> depending on your industry, revenue, data volume, and existing security posture. The $1,425 average is a reasonable ballpark for a small operation with basic controls in place.</p>

<p>Factors that push the price up:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Healthcare, legal, or financial industry (higher regulatory exposure)</li>
  <li>High transaction volume or large customer database</li>
  <li>No MFA or documented security practices</li>
  <li>Prior cyber incidents or claims</li>
</ul>

<p>Factors that can bring it down:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Strong security controls already in place</li>
  <li>Low revenue or limited data storage</li>
  <li>Bundling with your existing commercial lines (available with some carriers)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Does my general liability or BOP cover cyber attacks?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Almost always no, or only minimally. Standard GL and BOP policies were not designed to cover cyber events. At best, you may have a small sublimit for "electronic data" on a commercial property policy — typically $10,000 to $25,000 — which won't come close to covering the real cost of a breach. Standalone cyber insurance is the only product designed specifically for this exposure.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Is cyber insurance required by law in Michigan?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Not by a blanket state law, but many contracts and client agreements now require it — especially if you work with government entities, large corporations, or healthcare organizations. Michigan's data breach notification law (MCL 445.72) does require you to notify affected customers after a breach, and the compliance cost of that process alone makes a strong case for carrying coverage.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>What's the difference between cyber liability and data breach insurance?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>The terms are often used interchangeably, but technically: data breach insurance focuses on costs from a specific data exposure event (notification, credit monitoring, forensics). Cyber liability is broader — it also covers business interruption, ransomware, cyber extortion, and third-party liability claims. Most modern policies combine both, but always confirm what's included and what the sublimits are.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>My business is tiny — am I really a target?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Yes, more so than you might think. Automated attack tools don't discriminate by business size — they scan for vulnerabilities indiscriminately. Small businesses are often specifically attractive to cybercriminals because they tend to have weaker defenses than large companies. 43% of cyberattacks target small businesses. The fact that your business is small doesn't reduce your exposure; it often increases it relative to the security resources you have.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Can I get cyber insurance bundled with my existing business coverage?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Sometimes — some carriers offer cyber endorsements on a BOP or package policy. Whether that makes sense depends on the limits and terms of the endorsement compared to a standalone policy. In many cases, a standalone cyber policy provides meaningfully better coverage for similar premium. When we quote your coverage, we'll compare both options and show you the difference.</p>
  </div>
</details>
