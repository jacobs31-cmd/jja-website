<p class="lead">Most Michigan small business owners need at least two types of insurance from day one: general liability and commercial property. A Business Owner&#39;s Policy — called a BOP — bundles both into a single policy, usually at a lower combined price than buying them separately. Here&#39;s what&#39;s inside, what&#39;s not, and whether it&#39;s the right fit for your business.</p>

<div class="callout-box">
<p><strong>Who it&#39;s designed for:</strong> BOPs were built for small to mid-sized businesses with physical locations — retail stores, restaurants, offices, service businesses, salons, small professional firms. If your business has a space, serves customers, holds inventory, or owns equipment, a BOP is likely worth a look.</p>
</div>

<h2>What a BOP Includes</h2>

<p>A standard Business Owner&#39;s Policy bundles three core coverages:</p>

<h3>General Liability</h3>
<p>Covers third-party bodily injury and property damage claims arising from your business operations. A customer slips on your floor, a contractor damages a client&#39;s property, a product you sell injures someone — general liability responds to those claims and covers your legal defense. Most BOPs include $1,000,000 per occurrence / $2,000,000 aggregate as the standard GL limit.</p>

<h3>Commercial Property</h3>
<p>Covers your physical business assets — the building (if you own it), furniture, fixtures, equipment, inventory, and computers — against covered perils like fire, theft, vandalism, and certain weather events. If a fire destroys your retail space or a break-in takes your equipment, commercial property pays to repair or replace what was lost.</p>

<h3>Business Interruption Insurance</h3>
<p>This is the component most business owners forget about until they need it. If a covered loss forces you to close temporarily — a fire, a burst pipe, storm damage — business interruption coverage replaces the income you&#39;re losing while repairs happen. It also covers ongoing fixed expenses like rent and payroll that continue even when the doors are shut. For many small Michigan businesses, a forced closure without income replacement coverage is an existential threat.</p>

<h2>What a BOP Does NOT Cover</h2>

<p>A BOP is a strong foundation, but it&#39;s not complete business insurance by itself. Standard BOPs exclude:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Workers&#39; compensation</strong> — required separately in Michigan if you have employees meeting the state&#39;s threshold (3 or more employees, or 1 working 35+ hours/week for 13+ consecutive weeks)</li>
  <li><strong>Commercial auto</strong> — vehicles used for business need their own policy; personal auto coverage typically excludes business use</li>
  <li><strong>Professional liability (E&amp;O)</strong> — if your business provides professional advice or services, errors and omissions coverage is separate</li>
  <li><strong>Cyber liability</strong> — data breaches, ransomware, and cyber incidents require standalone cyber coverage</li>
  <li><strong>Flood and earthquake</strong> — just like homeowners policies, commercial property excludes flood damage; separate coverage is needed</li>
  <li><strong>Employment practices liability</strong> — claims related to discrimination, harassment, or wrongful termination</li>
</ul>

<h2>Who Qualifies for a BOP</h2>

<p>Not every business qualifies for a BOP. Carriers use specific eligibility criteria, and businesses that fall outside those parameters need a commercial package policy or individually structured coverage instead. BOP eligibility typically requires:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Annual revenue generally under $5–$10 million (varies by carrier)</li>
  <li>10,000 square feet of space or less at any one location (some carriers go higher)</li>
  <li>Operations considered low to moderate risk</li>
  <li>Industry type — most retail, office, and light service businesses qualify; contractors, manufacturers, and high-risk industries typically do not</li>
</ul>

<p>Businesses that don&#39;t qualify for a BOP aren&#39;t uninsurable — they just need their coverage structured differently. A commercial package policy builds similar coverage with more flexibility for businesses with complex or higher-risk operations.</p>

<h2>BOP vs. Separate Policies: Which Makes More Sense?</h2>

<p>For most qualifying small businesses, a BOP costs less than buying GL and commercial property separately — sometimes significantly less. The bundled structure also simplifies your coverage: one policy, one renewal date, one insurer to deal with if you have a claim.</p>

<p>The case for separate policies is typically when one component needs to be with a specialist carrier — for example, a restaurant with a complex property risk might need a specialty property carrier while keeping GL with a standard market. Your agent evaluates both options and shows you the comparison.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Does Michigan require a BOP or general liability for businesses?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Michigan law doesn&#39;t require most businesses to carry a BOP or general liability insurance. But practical requirements from landlords, clients, licensing boards, and municipalities effectively mandate it for most operating businesses. Commercial leases almost universally require a certificate of insurance with specified GL limits. If you&#39;re in a regulated industry or bidding on government contracts, coverage requirements are typically spelled out explicitly.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Can I add coverages to a BOP?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Yes — most carriers allow BOP endorsements that add coverage for specific needs. Common additions include equipment breakdown, cyber liability (at limited sublimits), employment practices liability, hired and non-owned auto, and liquor liability for businesses that serve alcohol. Whether an endorsement or a standalone policy is more appropriate depends on the coverage limit and your specific exposure. We&#39;ll walk through the options when we quote your coverage.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>My business operates out of my home. Do I need a BOP?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Your homeowners policy almost certainly excludes business activities — equipment, inventory, and liability arising from business operations are typically not covered. A home-based business often needs at minimum a home business endorsement on the homeowners policy or a separate BOP. The right answer depends on the nature of your business, how much equipment or inventory you have, and whether clients come to your home. Don&#39;t assume your personal homeowners coverage extends to your business — it almost certainly doesn&#39;t.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>What happens if my business has to close temporarily due to a covered loss?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>That&#39;s exactly what business interruption coverage handles. If a covered peril — fire, burst pipe, storm damage — forces you to close while repairs are made, business interruption pays for your lost revenue and continuing expenses during the closure period, up to your policy&#39;s limits and waiting period. The waiting period (typically 72 hours) means it kicks in after the first few days. Review your business interruption limits carefully — they should reflect your actual monthly revenue and fixed expense obligations, not a default low number.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>How is a BOP different from commercial package insurance?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>A BOP is a standardized, pre-packaged product designed for small, lower-risk businesses. Commercial package policies (CPPs) are more flexible and customizable, built for larger or more complex operations that don&#39;t fit the BOP eligibility box. CPPs can include the same core coverages as a BOP but allow for broader coverage options, higher limits, and specialty endorsements. If your business has outgrown BOP eligibility or has unique exposures, a commercial package is typically the right path.</p>
  </div>
</details>
