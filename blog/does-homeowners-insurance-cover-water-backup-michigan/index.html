Does Homeowners Insurance Cover Water Backup and Sump Pump Failure in Michigan?

By Joseph Jacobs·July 17, 2026·6 min read

Want to know if your basement is protected? Review your Michigan home insurance coverage →

Does homeowners insurance cover water backup and sump pump failure in Michigan? Not automatically. A standard Michigan homeowners policy excludes water that backs up through sewers or drains, and it excludes losses caused by a sump pump that fails or gets overwhelmed. To be covered, you need a specific add-on called water backup and sump overflow coverage — and most Michigan basements don't have it until after the first flooded finished basement teaches the lesson the hard way.

The short answer: Standard homeowners policy → not covered. Add the water backup / sump overflow endorsement → covered up to the limit you choose. This is separate from flood insurance, which covers a different kind of water entirely.

Why Your Standard Policy Doesn't Cover a Backed-Up Basement

Homeowners insurance is built to cover sudden and accidental water damage from inside the home — a burst pipe, an overflowing water heater, a supply line that lets go behind the washing machine. Those are covered on a standard Michigan policy.

But two of the most common ways a Michigan basement floods are specifically carved out of that coverage:

  • Water or sewage backing up through a drain or sewer. When a municipal sewer surcharges during heavy rain, or a floor drain backs up, the standard policy's water-backup exclusion applies.
  • Sump pump failure or overflow. When your sump pump dies, loses power in a storm, or simply can't keep up with the water table, the resulting damage is excluded.

Insurers exclude these because they're common, predictable, and tied to aging infrastructure and heavy weather — exactly the risks that need to be priced separately. The good news: buying that separate coverage is inexpensive.

What Water Backup Coverage Actually Covers

The endorsement covers damage when water or sewage backs up through sewers or drains, or overflows from a sump pump/sump pit that failed. In a typical finished Michigan basement, that means:

  • Structural cleanup and repair — drywall, flooring, trim, and insulation ruined by the water.
  • Personal property — furniture, electronics, stored belongings, and anything in the basement's living space.
  • Mechanical systems — the furnace, water heater, and washer/dryer if they're in the affected area.
  • Water removal and remediation — the pump-out and mold prevention that follow.

Coverage is capped at the limit you select — commonly offered in tiers like $5,000, $10,000, $25,000, $50,000, and $100,000. The endorsement carries its own deductible, and the limit you pick is the ceiling on the whole loss, so choose it based on what your basement would actually cost to restore.

How Much Water Backup Coverage Do You Need?

Match the limit to the basement:

  • Unfinished basement, minimal storage: $10,000 is a sensible minimum — you're covering cleanup plus a water heater, a furnace, and whatever you keep down there.
  • Finished basement with living space: $25,000 is a common floor. Between drywall, flooring, and furnishings, a finished-basement flood routinely runs well into five figures.
  • High-end finished basement (kitchenette, home theater, guest suite): $50,000 to $100,000 or more. A fully finished lower level with built-ins and mechanicals can easily exceed $100,000 to restore, and the higher limits cost surprisingly little more.

The premium is one of the best values on a homeowners policy relative to what it protects. As a rough guide, a $5,000 limit often runs around $80 a year, while a $50,000 limit can be closer to $300 or more, depending on the limit, your deductible, and your carrier. Given that a flooded finished basement can cost far more than that to restore, it's money well spent. If you've finished your basement since you bought your policy, this is the coverage most likely to be missing.

Water Backup vs. Flood Insurance — Not the Same Thing

This trips up a lot of Michigan homeowners, so it's worth being precise. Water backup coverage handles water that comes up through your drains, sewer line, or sump — from the inside out. Flood insurance handles surface water that rises and enters your home from the outside — an overflowing creek, a lake surge, water pooling across the yard and coming in at grade. Neither is included in a standard homeowners policy, and each requires its own separate coverage. If your home sits near water or in a low-lying area, you may want both — see our guide to flood insurance in Michigan for the other half of the picture.

The Bottom Line

If you have a finished basement in Michigan and you haven't specifically added water backup and sump overflow coverage, assume you don't have it — it's an opt-in endorsement, not a standard inclusion, and it's the single most common gap we find on Michigan home policies. Frequently Asked Questions

Does homeowners insurance cover a sump pump failure in Michigan?

Not on a standard policy. Damage from a sump pump that fails, loses power, or is overwhelmed is excluded unless you've added the water backup and sump overflow endorsement. With the endorsement, the resulting basement damage is covered up to your selected limit, subject to that endorsement's deductible.

What's the difference between water backup coverage and flood insurance?

Water backup covers water coming up through your sewers, drains, or sump pump — from inside your plumbing system. Flood insurance covers surface water rising and entering from outside. They're separate policies/endorsements, and a standard homeowners policy includes neither. Many Michigan homes near lakes or in low areas benefit from both.

How much does water backup coverage cost in Michigan?

As a rough guide, a $5,000 limit often runs around $80 a year, and a $50,000 limit can be closer to $300 or more — it varies with the limit, your deductible, and your carrier. Given that a flooded finished basement can cost well over $25,000 to restore, it's one of the best-value add-ons on a homeowners policy. We can quote it against your current policy in a few minutes.

My basement flooded during a storm — is that covered?

It depends on how the water got in. If a sewer or drain backed up, or your sump pump was overwhelmed, you need the water backup endorsement for coverage. If surface water rose and entered from outside, that's a flood and requires flood insurance. If a pipe burst inside the home, that's typically covered on the standard policy. Call us and we'll help you figure out which applies to your loss.

Should I get a battery backup sump pump too?

Yes — it's the best pairing with the coverage. Most sump failures in Michigan happen during storms that also knock out power, exactly when you need the pump most. A battery backup pump reduces the odds of a claim, and the insurance endorsement covers you if it still isn't enough. Prevention plus coverage is the right combination for a finished basement.
