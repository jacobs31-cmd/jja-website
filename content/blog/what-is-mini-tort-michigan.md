<p class="lead">What is mini-tort in Michigan? It's a narrow exception to the state's no-fault system that lets you recover <strong>up to $3,000</strong> in vehicle damage from the driver who caused your accident — most often to get back the collision deductible you had to pay. It only covers damage to your car, not injuries, and you can only use it if you were <strong>less than 50% at fault</strong>. It's one of the most misunderstood pieces of Michigan auto law, and knowing how it works can put real money back in your pocket after a crash.</p>

<div class="callout-box">
<p><strong>The short answer:</strong> Michigan is a no-fault state, so normally you can't sue the other driver over a car accident. The <strong>mini-tort</strong> is the exception that lets you recover up to <strong>$3,000</strong> for vehicle damage from an at-fault driver — as long as you were <strong>less than 50% at fault</strong>. It does <strong>not</strong> cover injuries.</p>
</div>

<h2>Why Michigan Needs a "Mini-Tort" at All</h2>

<p>Under Michigan's no-fault system, your own insurance handles your injuries (through <a href="../michigan-no-fault-option-6/">PIP coverage</a>) and typically your own vehicle damage (through collision coverage) — regardless of who caused the crash. The trade-off is that you generally <strong>can't sue</strong> the other driver for a car accident.</p>

<p>But that leaves a gap: what about the collision deductible you had to pay out of pocket, even though the crash wasn't your fault? Or the damage to your car if you didn't carry collision coverage at all? The mini-tort — formally part of Michigan's no-fault statute — is the limited right to sue that fills exactly that gap, capped at $3,000.</p>

<h2>What Mini-Tort Covers (and What It Doesn't)</h2>

<p>The mini-tort is deliberately narrow. Here's the line:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Covered:</strong> Damage to your vehicle that your own insurance didn't pay — most commonly your <strong>collision deductible</strong>. If you had no collision coverage, it can cover up to $3,000 of your actual repair cost.</li>
  <li><strong>Covered:</strong> Vehicle-related property inside or on the car that was damaged (within the $3,000 cap).</li>
  <li><strong>NOT covered:</strong> Any bodily injury — that runs through no-fault PIP, never mini-tort.</li>
  <li><strong>NOT covered:</strong> Anything above $3,000. The cap is firm, even if your uncovered damage was higher.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The $3,000 Limit — Raised From $1,000</h2>

<p>The mini-tort maximum was <strong>$1,000 for decades</strong>, then raised to <strong>$3,000</strong> as part of Michigan's 2019 no-fault reform (effective for accidents from July 2020 onward). That's a meaningful jump — a $3,000 recovery can fully reimburse a high collision deductible or cover the whole repair on an older car you insured for liability only. Make sure any older guidance you read reflects the current $3,000 figure, not the outdated $1,000 one.</p>

<h2>The "Less Than 50% at Fault" Rule</h2>

<p>This is the requirement that decides whether you can collect at all. To recover under the mini-tort, you must be <strong>less than 50% at fault</strong> for the accident. Michigan's Department of Insurance and Financial Services (DIFS) puts it plainly: if you're less than 50% at fault, you can pursue the other driver for your deductible; if you're more than 50% at fault, the other driver can pursue you instead.</p>

<p>Your recovery is also reduced by your share of the fault. If you're found 20% at fault on a $3,000 claim, you can recover up to $2,400 (80%). This is why the police report and clear documentation of what happened matter so much for a mini-tort claim.</p>

<h2>How to File a Mini-Tort Claim in Michigan</h2>

<ol>
  <li><strong>File your own collision claim first.</strong> If you carry collision coverage, let your insurer repair the car; you'll pay your deductible. The mini-tort is how you get that deductible back.</li>
  <li><strong>Get the at-fault driver's insurance information</strong> and the police report or crash documentation establishing who was at fault.</li>
  <li><strong>Send a written mini-tort demand</strong> to the at-fault driver's insurance company, stating the amount (your deductible, or your uncovered repair cost up to $3,000) and including your repair estimate/invoice and proof of the deductible you paid.</li>
  <li><strong>Follow up.</strong> Mini-tort claims are small and usually settle without a lawsuit; if the insurer won't pay, the claim can go to small claims or district court.</li>
  <li><strong>Don't wait.</strong> Michigan property-damage claims are subject to a filing deadline — pursue the mini-tort promptly after your accident rather than letting it sit.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>How much can you recover with a Michigan mini-tort claim?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Up to $3,000 in vehicle damage from the at-fault driver, reduced by your own percentage of fault. The limit was raised from $1,000 to $3,000 under Michigan's 2019 no-fault reform, effective for accidents on or after July 1, 2020. Mini-tort never covers bodily injury — only vehicle damage.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Do I qualify for mini-tort if the accident was partly my fault?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Only if you were less than 50% at fault. If you were 50% or more at fault, you can't recover under the mini-tort (and the other driver may pursue you). If you were partially at fault but under 50%, your recovery is reduced by your share — for example, 30% at fault means you can recover up to 70% of the eligible amount.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Does mini-tort cover my car repairs if I don't have collision coverage?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Yes, up to the $3,000 cap. If you insured your vehicle for liability only and had no collision coverage, the mini-tort lets you recover up to $3,000 of your repair cost from the at-fault driver — but not a dollar more, even if the damage exceeded that. For a newer or higher-value vehicle, that's a strong reason to carry collision coverage rather than rely on mini-tort.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Is mini-tort the same as suing for my injuries?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>No. Mini-tort covers vehicle damage only, capped at $3,000. Injury claims in Michigan are handled through no-fault PIP coverage, and the separate right to sue an at-fault driver for serious injury is a different part of the law with its own high threshold. Don't confuse the two — mini-tort is strictly about your car.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Who do I file the mini-tort claim against?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>The at-fault driver's auto insurance company. You send them a written demand for your uncovered vehicle damage (your deductible or up to $3,000 of repair cost), with your documentation. Most claims settle directly; if not, small claims or district court is the next step.</p>
  </div>
</details>
