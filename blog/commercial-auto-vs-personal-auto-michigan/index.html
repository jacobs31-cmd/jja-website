Commercial Auto vs. Personal Auto Insurance in Michigan: Why the Difference Matters

By Joseph Jacobs·May 25, 2026·7 min read

Need commercial auto coverage for your Michigan business? Get a free commercial insurance quote →

You drive your personal vehicle to job sites, client appointments, or deliveries. You've had the same auto policy for years. What you may not know: if you're in an accident while using that vehicle for business purposes, your personal auto policy might deny the claim entirely. That gap catches Michigan workers and business owners off guard — often at the worst possible moment.

The core issue: Personal auto policies are written to cover personal use. Most contain a business use exclusion — language that voids coverage for accidents that occur during commercial activity. The line between personal and business use isn't always obvious, and insurers draw it tightly when a claim is filed.

What Personal Auto Insurance Covers (and Doesn't)

Your personal auto policy covers you driving to the grocery store, taking the kids to school, commuting to a fixed workplace, and personal errands. Most policies extend to occasional incidental business use — stopping at a client's office once in a while. Where coverage breaks down is consistent business use: regularly transporting clients, driving between job sites, hauling equipment or materials, or using the vehicle as an essential tool of your trade.

Michigan's no-fault system means your own PIP (Personal Injury Protection) coverage handles your medical bills regardless of fault — that piece generally follows you. But liability coverage — what protects you when you injure someone else or damage their property — is where business use exclusions do their damage. A denied liability claim on a $400,000 injury lawsuit means that exposure comes out of your pocket personally.

Who Needs Commercial Auto Insurance in Michigan

The list is longer than most people expect:

Contractors and tradespeople

If you're driving a truck or van between job sites, hauling tools and materials, or using your vehicle as a mobile workshop, that's business use. Electricians, plumbers, HVAC techs, painters, landscapers — the vehicle isn't just transportation, it's part of the operation. A personal policy won't cover a claim that happens on the way to a job or while your truck is loaded with work equipment.

Real estate agents and insurance agents

Driving clients to showings, traveling between listings, or making regular client visits qualifies as business use in most insurers' underwriting guidelines. Michigan realtors in particular rack up significant business mileage — most personal policies aren't written to handle that exposure.

Delivery and transportation workers

Food delivery, courier services, and any transport-for-hire falls squarely into commercial territory. Personal policies almost universally exclude this category. Michigan gig economy workers using their personal vehicles for DoorDash, Amazon Flex, or similar platforms are in a gray zone — rideshare and delivery endorsements exist but often provide narrow coverage. A dedicated commercial auto policy provides cleaner, more complete protection.

Sales representatives and field service workers

If your job requires regular travel to customers, prospects, or field locations and your employer doesn't provide a company vehicle, your personal vehicle is doing commercial work. Whether your employer's liability coverage extends to your vehicle in an accident varies — and assuming it does is a dangerous assumption.

Business owners who use one vehicle for everything

Many Michigan small business owners drive the same vehicle for personal errands and business activity. "I only use it for work sometimes" doesn't protect you — if you're in an accident on a business errand, the use at that moment is what the claim adjuster looks at, not the overall ratio of personal to business miles.

What Commercial Auto Insurance Actually Covers

Commercial auto policies are built for business use situations that personal policies aren't. Key differences:

Personal Auto Commercial Auto
Business use coveredLimited / excludedYes
Higher liability limits availableLimitedYes — up to $1M+
Multiple drivers / employees coveredHousehold onlyYes
Tools and equipment in vehicleNot coveredAdd-on available
Hired and non-owned autoNot availableAvailable

Hired and non-owned auto (HNOA) is worth calling out specifically. This coverage protects your business when employees use their personal vehicles on company business. If your employee is in an accident while running a business errand in their own car, your company can be named in the resulting lawsuit. HNOA coverage on your commercial auto or BOP protects against that exposure.

What Happens if You Have a Business Accident on a Personal Policy

The scenario plays out like this: you're driving to a job site, client visit, or delivery and get into an at-fault accident. The other driver is injured and files a claim against your liability coverage. Your personal insurer investigates, determines you were engaged in business activity at the time, and denies the claim based on the business use exclusion in your policy.

You're now personally liable for the other driver's medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, and property damage — without insurance to cover it. In Michigan, serious injury claims can reach six figures or more. That exposure sits entirely on you.

Beyond the liability denial, your personal insurer may also have grounds to cancel your policy for material misrepresentation if they determine you've been regularly using the vehicle for commercial purposes without disclosing it.

Michigan note: Michigan's no-fault PIP coverage does follow you as a Michigan resident in most situations — your own injury coverage is more portable. The business use exclusion hits hardest on the liability side, which is where the real financial exposure lives for most Michigan workers and business owners.

What Does Commercial Auto Insurance Cost in Michigan?

Commercial auto premiums in Michigan depend on the vehicle type, primary use, annual mileage, driver history, and the coverage limits you carry. General ranges:

Yes, commercial auto costs more than a personal policy — because it provides meaningfully more protection for business use situations. The cost of a denied claim on a serious accident dwarfs any premium savings from skipping commercial coverage.

For some lower-risk business uses — a realtor who drives clients occasionally, for example — a business use endorsement on an existing personal policy may be a cost-effective middle ground. We'll look at your specific situation and show you the options.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I just add a business use endorsement to my personal auto policy?

Sometimes, for lighter business use. Many Michigan carriers offer a business use endorsement that extends personal auto coverage to include regular business driving — client visits, sales travel, occasional job site trips. It won't cover higher-risk commercial activity like hauling heavy loads, transporting passengers for hire, or using the vehicle as a primary work tool. If your business use is moderate and low-risk, this endorsement can bridge the gap at a lower cost than a full commercial policy. We'll tell you honestly whether it's enough for your situation.

My employer says I'm covered under their policy when I use my car for work. Is that true?

Possibly — but verify it before you rely on it. Some employers carry hired and non-owned auto (HNOA) coverage that extends liability protection to employees using personal vehicles on company business. But HNOA typically covers the employer's liability, not your personal vehicle damage. If your car is totaled in a business-use accident, your employer's policy likely won't pay to replace it — your personal comprehensive and collision coverage would need to respond, and that's where the business use exclusion can create problems. Get the specifics of your employer's coverage in writing before assuming you're fully protected.

I use my truck for work but it's also my personal vehicle. Do I need two policies?

No — you don't need two separate policies. A commercial auto policy can cover a vehicle used for both personal and business purposes. You'd simply insure it under a commercial policy with appropriate coverage for both use types. In many cases this is simpler and cleaner than trying to maintain a personal policy with a business use endorsement that may have coverage gaps. We'll look at your usage and recommend the right structure.

Does commercial auto cover my tools and equipment in the truck?

Standard commercial auto covers the vehicle itself — not the contents. Tools, equipment, and materials stored in or on the vehicle need separate coverage, typically an inland marine or tools-and-equipment policy. This is one of the most common coverage gaps for Michigan contractors: the truck is insured, but $15,000 worth of tools in the bed are not. Ask about tools and equipment coverage when we're putting together your commercial auto — it's usually an affordable add-on.

What's the difference between commercial auto and a fleet policy?

A fleet policy is simply a commercial auto policy that covers multiple vehicles under one policy — typically three or more. It offers administrative convenience (one renewal, one certificate) and sometimes better per-vehicle pricing at scale. If your Michigan business has grown to the point of running two or more work vehicles, it's worth asking whether a fleet structure makes sense. We can show you the comparison.
