<p class="lead">You drive your personal vehicle to job sites, client appointments, or deliveries. You&#39;ve had the same auto policy for years. What you may not know: if you&#39;re in an accident while using that vehicle for business purposes, your personal auto policy might deny the claim entirely. That gap catches Michigan workers and business owners off guard — often at the worst possible moment.</p>

<div class="callout-box">
<p><strong>The core issue:</strong> Personal auto policies are written to cover personal use. Most contain a business use exclusion — language that voids coverage for accidents that occur during commercial activity. The line between personal and business use isn&#39;t always obvious, and insurers draw it tightly when a claim is filed.</p>
</div>

<h2>What Personal Auto Insurance Covers (and Doesn&#39;t)</h2>

<p>Your personal auto policy covers you driving to the grocery store, taking the kids to school, commuting to a fixed workplace, and personal errands. Most policies extend to occasional incidental business use — stopping at a client&#39;s office once in a while. Where coverage breaks down is consistent business use: regularly transporting clients, driving between job sites, hauling equipment or materials, or using the vehicle as an essential tool of your trade.</p>

<p>Michigan&#39;s no-fault system means your own PIP (Personal Injury Protection) coverage handles your medical bills regardless of fault — that piece generally follows you. But liability coverage — what protects you when you injure someone else or damage their property — is where business use exclusions do their damage. A denied liability claim on a $400,000 injury lawsuit means that exposure comes out of your pocket personally.</p>

<h2>Who Needs Commercial Auto Insurance in Michigan</h2>

<p>The list is longer than most people expect:</p>

<h3>Contractors and tradespeople</h3>
<p>If you&#39;re driving a truck or van between job sites, hauling tools and materials, or using your vehicle as a mobile workshop, that&#39;s business use. Electricians, plumbers, HVAC techs, painters, landscapers — the vehicle isn&#39;t just transportation, it&#39;s part of the operation. A personal policy won&#39;t cover a claim that happens on the way to a job or while your truck is loaded with work equipment.</p>

<h3>Real estate agents and insurance agents</h3>
<p>Driving clients to showings, traveling between listings, or making regular client visits qualifies as business use in most insurers&#39; underwriting guidelines. Michigan realtors in particular rack up significant business mileage — most personal policies aren&#39;t written to handle that exposure.</p>

<h3>Delivery and transportation workers</h3>
<p>Food delivery, courier services, and any transport-for-hire falls squarely into commercial territory. Personal policies almost universally exclude this category. Michigan gig economy workers using their personal vehicles for DoorDash, Amazon Flex, or similar platforms are in a gray zone — rideshare and delivery endorsements exist but often provide narrow coverage. A dedicated commercial auto policy provides cleaner, more complete protection.</p>

<h3>Sales representatives and field service workers</h3>
<p>If your job requires regular travel to customers, prospects, or field locations and your employer doesn&#39;t provide a company vehicle, your personal vehicle is doing commercial work. Whether your employer&#39;s liability coverage extends to your vehicle in an accident varies — and assuming it does is a dangerous assumption.</p>

<h3>Business owners who use one vehicle for everything</h3>
<p>Many Michigan small business owners drive the same vehicle for personal errands and business activity. "I only use it for work sometimes" doesn&#39;t protect you — if you&#39;re in an accident on a business errand, the use at that moment is what the claim adjuster looks at, not the overall ratio of personal to business miles.</p>

HNOA coverage on your commercial auto or BOP protects against that exposure.</p>

<h2>What Happens if You Have a Business Accident on a Personal Policy</h2>

<p>The scenario plays out like this: you&#39;re driving to a job site, client visit, or delivery and get into an at-fault accident. The other driver is injured and files a claim against your liability coverage. Your personal insurer investigates, determines you were engaged in business activity at the time, and denies the claim based on the business use exclusion in your policy.</p>

<p>You&#39;re now personally liable for the other driver&#39;s medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, and property damage — without insurance to cover it. In Michigan, serious injury claims can reach six figures or more. That exposure sits entirely on you.</p>

<p>Beyond the liability denial, your personal insurer may also have grounds to cancel your policy for material misrepresentation if they determine you&#39;ve been regularly using the vehicle for commercial purposes without disclosing it.</p>

<div class="callout-box">
<p><strong>Michigan note:</strong> Michigan&#39;s no-fault PIP coverage does follow you as a Michigan resident in most situations — your own injury coverage is more portable. The business use exclusion hits hardest on the liability side, which is where the real financial exposure lives for most Michigan workers and business owners.</p>
</div>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Can I just add a business use endorsement to my personal auto policy?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Sometimes, for lighter business use. Many Michigan carriers offer a business use endorsement that extends personal auto coverage to include regular business driving — client visits, sales travel, occasional job site trips. It won&#39;t cover higher-risk commercial activity like hauling heavy loads, transporting passengers for hire, or using the vehicle as a primary work tool. If your business use is moderate and low-risk, this endorsement can bridge the gap at a lower cost than a full commercial policy. We&#39;ll tell you honestly whether it&#39;s enough for your situation.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>My employer says I&#39;m covered under their policy when I use my car for work. Is that true?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Possibly — but verify it before you rely on it. Some employers carry hired and non-owned auto (HNOA) coverage that extends liability protection to employees using personal vehicles on company business. But HNOA typically covers the employer&#39;s liability, not your personal vehicle damage. If your car is totaled in a business-use accident, your employer&#39;s policy likely won&#39;t pay to replace it — your personal comprehensive and collision coverage would need to respond, and that&#39;s where the business use exclusion can create problems. Get the specifics of your employer&#39;s coverage in writing before assuming you&#39;re fully protected.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>I use my truck for work but it&#39;s also my personal vehicle. Do I need two policies?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>No — you don&#39;t need two separate policies. A commercial auto policy can cover a vehicle used for both personal and business purposes. You&#39;d simply insure it under a commercial policy with appropriate coverage for both use types. In many cases this is simpler and cleaner than trying to maintain a personal policy with a business use endorsement that may have coverage gaps. We&#39;ll look at your usage and recommend the right structure.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Does commercial auto cover my tools and equipment in the truck?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Standard commercial auto covers the vehicle itself — not the contents. Tools, equipment, and materials stored in or on the vehicle need separate coverage, typically an inland marine or tools-and-equipment policy. This is one of the most common coverage gaps for Michigan contractors: the truck is insured, but $15,000 worth of tools in the bed are not. Ask about tools and equipment coverage when we&#39;re putting together your commercial auto — it&#39;s usually an affordable add-on.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>What&#39;s the difference between commercial auto and a fleet policy?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>A fleet policy is simply a commercial auto policy that covers multiple vehicles under one policy — typically three or more. It offers administrative convenience (one renewal, one certificate) and sometimes better per-vehicle pricing at scale. If your Michigan business has grown to the point of running two or more work vehicles, it&#39;s worth asking whether a fleet structure makes sense. We can show you the comparison.</p>
  </div>
</details>
