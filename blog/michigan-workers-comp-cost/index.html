How Much Does Workers' Comp Cost in Michigan?

By Joseph Jacobs·June 12, 2026·6 min read

Workers' compensation is required for most Michigan employers, so the real question isn't whether you need it — it's what it should cost. The good news: Michigan is one of the most affordable states in the country for workers' comp. The catch: because Michigan lets every insurer set its own rates, two carriers can quote the same business wildly different numbers. Here's how the price actually works in 2026.

How workers' comp is priced

Workers' comp isn't a flat premium — it's calculated from your payroll. The basic formula is:

(Payroll ÷ 100) × your class-code rate × your experience modifier.

Every type of work has a classification code with its own rate per $100 of payroll, because the risk of injury varies enormously. Clerical and office work is cheap to insure; roofing and framing are not.

What that means in real Michigan numbers

Michigan rates are among the lowest in the U.S. — averaging well under $1.50 per $100 of payroll, roughly 22% below the national median. For context, the national average is around $1.03 per $100 of payroll. Some real examples of how much classification matters:

  • A retail or clerical class can run well under $1 per $100 of payroll — often closer to $0.78.
  • A residential carpenter might pay roughly $1.99 per $100 of payroll — more than double the office rate.
  • Higher-hazard trades (roofing, structural work) climb from there.

So a contractor with $300,000 of carpentry payroll at about $1.99 per $100 would land near $6,000 a year before credits — while a small office with the same payroll could pay a fraction of that.

Why Michigan is different (and why shopping matters so much)

Since 1983, Michigan has used an open-competition system: the state doesn't set workers' comp rates — each insurance company files its own. That's great for businesses, but only if you actually compare. The identical business, with the identical class codes, can get quotes that differ by hundreds or thousands of dollars depending on which carriers want that risk this year. A single quote tells you almost nothing about whether you're paying a fair price.

What drives your number up or down

  • Class codes. Getting your payroll classified correctly is the single biggest factor — and misclassification is the most common reason Michigan businesses overpay.
  • Experience modifier (your "Mod"). Once your business is large enough to be rated, your claims history becomes a multiplier. A clean record drops your premium; a bad year can raise it for three.
  • Payroll. More payroll means more premium, since it's the base of the whole calculation.
  • Safety and claims management. Documented safety programs and how you handle claims both feed back into your Mod over time.

How to pay less without cutting protection

  • Audit your class codes. Make sure clerical payroll isn't lumped in with field labor — that mistake alone can inflate a premium by thousands.
  • Protect your Mod. Report and manage claims promptly; a clean loss history compounds into lower rates year after year.
  • Don't skip the year-end audit. Estimating payroll wrong cuts both ways — see what happens if you skip your workers' comp audit.
  • Shop the whole market. In an open-rating state like Michigan, this is where the real savings live.

The bottom line

Michigan is a cheap state for workers' comp, but "cheap on average" doesn't mean you're getting the best rate — your class codes, your Mod, and which carrier you're with decide that.
