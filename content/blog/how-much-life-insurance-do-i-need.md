<p class="lead">Most people who buy life insurance either guess at a round number or accept whatever the agent suggests. Both approaches usually leave families underinsured. Here&#39;s how to actually calculate what you need — based on your real situation, not a generic formula.</p>

<div class="callout-box">
<p><strong>The starting point:</strong> Life insurance exists to replace what your family would lose if you were gone — your income, your mortgage payoff, your kids&#39; education, and the debts you&#39;d leave behind. The right coverage amount is the number that keeps your family&#39;s financial situation intact. Everything else is a guess.</p>
</div>

<h2>Why Most People Get the Number Wrong</h2>

<p>Two things drive underinsurance in Michigan families. First, people anchor to employer-provided coverage — typically 1–2x annual salary — and treat it as sufficient. It almost never is. Second, round numbers feel right: "I&#39;ll get a $500,000 policy." But $500,000 for a 35-year-old with a $300,000 mortgage, two kids, and a $90,000 income leaves a significant gap.</p>

<p>The goal isn&#39;t to hit a number that sounds big. It&#39;s to replace what your family actually depends on.</p>

<h2>The DIME Method: The Most Practical Calculator</h2>

<p>DIME stands for Debt, Income, Mortgage, and Education. Add those four numbers together and you have a solid, defensible coverage target.</p>

<h3>A Michigan example</h3>
<p>A 38-year-old Michigan homeowner earning $85,000 per year, married with two kids (ages 6 and 9), with a $240,000 mortgage balance, $30,000 in other debt, and two kids to put through college:</p>

<p>A round $500,000 policy — which many people would consider "pretty good" — covers less than a third of this family&#39;s actual need. That gap doesn&#39;t disappear because the policy felt like a big number when they bought it.</p>

<h2>Subtract What You Already Have</h2>

<p>Your DIME total isn&#39;t your final coverage number — it&#39;s your target. From it, subtract:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Existing life insurance (employer-provided + any personal policies already in force)</li>
  <li>Liquid savings and investments your family could access (not retirement accounts, which are harder to access)</li>
  <li>A spouse&#39;s income, if they work and would continue to</li>
</ul>

<p>What remains is your actual coverage gap — the amount of life insurance you should add.</p>

<h2>Don&#39;t Forget the Stay-at-Home Parent</h2>

<p>One of the most common miscalculations Michigan families make: insuring only the income-earning spouse. A stay-at-home parent&#39;s contribution — childcare, household management, transportation, education support — has real economic value. Replacing those services if that parent died would cost tens of thousands of dollars per year. That exposure needs coverage too, typically $250,000 to $500,000 depending on the family&#39;s situation.</p>

<h2>How Coverage Needs Change Over Time</h2>

<p>Life insurance isn&#39;t a set-it-and-forget-it purchase. Your need changes as your life does:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Mortgage paid off</strong> → that component of your need decreases</li>
  <li><strong>Kids become financially independent</strong> → income replacement period shortens</li>
  <li><strong>Retirement savings grow</strong> → assets offset some of the need</li>
  <li><strong>New child or dependent</strong> → need increases</li>
  <li><strong>Business ownership</strong> → may add buy-sell or key person needs</li>
</ul>

<p>Revisiting your coverage every five years — or after any major life change — keeps you from being either overinsured (paying for coverage you no longer need) or underinsured (carrying a policy that looked right ten years ago but doesn&#39;t reflect your current situation).</p>

<div class="callout-box">
<p><strong>Life insurance for business owners:</strong> If you own a Michigan business, your personal life insurance calculation doesn&#39;t capture your full exposure. Business debt, a buy-sell agreement with a partner, and key person replacement costs are separate coverage needs that sit on top of your personal family protection. It&#39;s worth having both conversations at the same time.</p>
</div>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Is the 10x income rule a good guideline?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>It&#39;s a quick starting point, not a precise answer. 10x income gets you in the right ballpark for many families — but it ignores your actual mortgage balance, debt, number of dependents, and existing assets. Two people earning the same income can have very different coverage needs based on their financial situations. The DIME method gives you a more accurate number in about ten minutes, and it&#39;s worth doing before you buy.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>How much does $1 million in term life insurance cost in Michigan?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>For a healthy Michigan resident in their 30s or early 40s, a 20-year $1 million term policy typically runs $40–$70 per month. Rates vary by age, health, gender, tobacco use, and carrier. The earlier you buy, the lower your rate — and that rate is locked for the full term. Getting a quote costs nothing and takes minutes.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Should I include retirement accounts in my coverage calculation?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Retirement accounts like 401(k)s and IRAs can be listed as beneficiaries and would pass to your family, but they come with withdrawal taxes and penalties if accessed before retirement age. For the DIME calculation, it&#39;s more conservative — and safer — to focus on liquid assets your family could use without penalty. If your retirement accounts are substantial, factor them in as a partial offset, but don&#39;t let them give you false confidence about being adequately covered.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>What if I can&#39;t afford the full coverage amount I need?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Start with what you can afford and increase it as your budget allows. Some coverage is dramatically better than none. Term life is inexpensive enough that most families can get meaningful protection within a reasonable budget — especially if they buy while they&#39;re young and healthy. A $500,000 policy isn&#39;t perfect if your need is $1.5 million, but it&#39;s far better than nothing, and you can layer on additional coverage later if your situation and budget allow.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Can I have multiple life insurance policies?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Yes — and it&#39;s actually a common and sensible strategy. Many Michigan families carry a base term policy for income replacement, an employer-provided group policy, and sometimes a separate smaller policy for mortgage protection or to cover a specific debt. As long as the total coverage is proportionate to your legitimate financial need and you disclose existing policies when applying, there&#39;s no issue with holding multiple policies from different carriers.</p>
  </div>
</details>
