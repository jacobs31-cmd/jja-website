High-Value Home Insurance in Michigan: What Standard Policies Miss

By Joseph Jacobs·July 12, 2026·8 min read

We insure higher-value Michigan homes across carriers that specialize in them. Get a free high-value home insurance review →

High-value home insurance in Michigan exists because a standard homeowners policy is built for the average house — and if your home is anything but average, the standard form can quietly leave you underinsured. Whether you own a custom lakefront build, a fully renovated historic home, or a newer home packed with upgrades, the gaps only surface at the worst possible time: after a fire, a burst pipe, or a liability claim. Here's what standard policies miss, and what a well-appointed Michigan home actually needs.

What counts as "high-value"? It's not about the price you paid — it's about the cost to rebuild and the value of what's inside. As a rule of thumb, if your home's replacement cost is roughly $750,000 or more, or it has custom finishes, high-end kitchens, extensive landscaping, or hard-to-replace features, it belongs in a policy designed for it — not a one-size-fits-all form.

What Makes a Home "High-Value" in Michigan

Insurers don't rate high-value homes off the same tables they use for a builder-grade subdivision house. The features that make your home special are the same ones that make it expensive to rebuild correctly:

  • Custom construction and finishes. Stone, hardwood, plaster, custom millwork, and specialty roofing cost far more to replace than standard materials — and a standard policy may not budget for them.
  • High-end kitchens and baths. Professional-grade appliances, natural stone, and custom cabinetry can run into six figures on their own.
  • Valuable contents. Jewelry, art, wine, firearms, antiques, and collectibles carry strict sub-limits on a standard policy.
  • Waterfront and acreage. Michigan lake homes, docks, boathouses, and outbuildings add exposures a basic policy handles poorly.
  • Higher liability exposure. Pools, guest space, staff, and simply having more assets to protect all raise the stakes on the liability side.

Where Standard Homeowners Policies Fall Short

A standard Michigan homeowners policy isn't "bad" — it's just designed around typical risks and typical price points. On a higher-value home, five gaps show up again and again.

1. Replacement cost caps

Many standard policies pay only up to your stated dwelling limit, or add a modest "extended replacement cost" cushion of 25% or so. After a total loss — when construction costs spike and everyone in the neighborhood is rebuilding at once — that cushion can fall short. High-value carriers often offer guaranteed or extended replacement cost that rebuilds your home to what it was, even if the final bill exceeds the policy limit.

2. Sub-limits on valuables

Standard policies cap categories like jewelry, watches, furs, silverware, and firearms at surprisingly low numbers — often $1,500 to $2,500 for theft. A single ring or a collection can blow past that instantly. The fix is scheduling those items or carrying a high-value contents form with broader limits and fewer exclusions.

3. Liability limits that don't match your assets

Most homeowners carry $300,000 to $500,000 in personal liability. For a household with real assets, that's thin. One serious injury claim or a lawsuit can exceed it — and the shortfall comes out of your pocket. Higher base limits plus a personal umbrella policy are standard practice for high-value homeowners.

4. Water backup and service-line gaps

Sewer and drain backup is excluded on a base policy unless you add it, and the default limits are often low. In Michigan — with our freeze-thaw cycles, spring melts, and aging service lines — a finished basement makes this one of the most common expensive claims. High-value programs typically offer far higher backup and service-line limits.

5. Loss-of-use limits that run out

If a covered loss forces you out of your home, your policy pays for temporary living expenses — but standard policies cap that "loss of use" coverage at a set percentage of your dwelling limit or a fixed time period. A high-value home built with custom materials can take well over a year to rebuild, long enough to exhaust a standard allowance. High-value policies offer much higher, and sometimes open-ended, loss-of-use limits so a long rebuild doesn't come out of your pocket.

Coverages a High-Value Michigan Home Should Have

When we build a program for a higher-value home, these are the pieces we make sure are in place:

  • Guaranteed or extended replacement cost on the dwelling, so a total loss rebuilds the home you actually have.
  • Scheduled personal property for jewelry, art, wine, and collectibles — with agreed value and no deductible on scheduled items.
  • Higher liability limits plus an umbrella to protect your assets against a serious claim.
  • Generous water backup and service-line coverage for finished basements and older utility lines.
  • Ordinance or law coverage so bringing an older Michigan home up to current building code after a loss doesn't come out of your pocket.
  • Coverage for outbuildings, docks, and detached structures at limits that reflect what they'd actually cost to rebuild.

Michigan-Specific Considerations

A few things make higher-value coverage especially worth reviewing in our state. Michigan weather — hail, ice dams, wind, and spring flooding — drives frequent and expensive claims, and rising rebuild costs have pushed premiums up statewide. Michigan's many lakefront and lakeshore homes tend to be higher-value properties with exposures a standard policy handles poorly — docks, boathouses, detached structures, and shoreline features that need their own limits. And flood is never covered by a homeowners policy of any tier — waterfront and low-lying properties need a separate flood policy. None of these are reasons to over-insure; they're reasons to have the right limits reviewed by someone who works with carriers built for these homes.

The Bottom Line

If your home is worth substantially more than the average house on your street, the average policy probably isn't the right fit. The good news is that fixing it rarely costs as much as owners expect — high-value carriers price these homes competitively because well-maintained, higher-value homes tend to be lower-risk. Frequently Asked Questions

What is considered a high-value home in Michigan?

There's no single legal threshold, but insurers generally treat a home as high-value when its rebuild cost is roughly $750,000 or more, or when it has custom construction, high-end finishes, or valuable contents that a standard policy can't properly cover. What matters is the cost to rebuild and replace what's inside — not the market price you paid.

Do I need a special policy, or can I just raise the limits on my standard homeowners insurance?

You can raise some limits, but many gaps — like low sub-limits on valuables, capped rebuild costs, and limited loss-of-use coverage — are built into the standard policy form and can't simply be dialed up. A dedicated high-value policy is designed differently from the ground up, which is why it usually protects a higher-end home better, and often at a competitive price.

Does high-value home insurance cover jewelry and art?

A standard policy caps categories like jewelry, watches, and art at low limits for theft. High-value policies let you schedule those items at agreed values with broader coverage and often no deductible, or provide much higher blanket limits. If you own valuable pieces, scheduling them is one of the most important upgrades you can make.

Is flooding covered on a high-value home policy in Michigan?

No. Flood is excluded on homeowners policies at every price point — high-value included. Waterfront, lakeshore, and low-lying Michigan homes need a separate flood policy through the NFIP or a private flood insurer. We can quote that alongside your home coverage so there's no gap.

Will a high-value policy cost a lot more than what I have now?

Not always. Because well-maintained, higher-value homes tend to be lower-risk, carriers that specialize in them price competitively — and bundling your home, auto, and umbrella often brings the total down. The only way to know is to compare your current declarations page against a specialty carrier, which is exactly what we do for Michigan homeowners at no cost.
