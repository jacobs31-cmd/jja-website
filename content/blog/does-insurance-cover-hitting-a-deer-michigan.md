<p class="lead">Does insurance cover hitting a deer in Michigan? Yes — but only if your auto policy includes <strong>comprehensive coverage</strong>. Hitting an animal is classified as a comprehensive claim, not a collision claim. If you carry only the state-required liability coverage, damage to your own vehicle from a deer strike comes out of your pocket. With more than 58,000 vehicle-deer crashes reported in Michigan in 2024, it's a question worth answering before it happens to you.</p>

<div class="callout-box">
<p><strong>The short answer:</strong> Hit the deer → <strong>comprehensive</strong> pays (minus your deductible). Swerve to avoid the deer and hit a tree, guardrail, or another car → that's a <strong>collision</strong> claim instead. Injuries to you or your passengers → your Michigan <strong>PIP (Personal Injury Protection)</strong> medical coverage, regardless of which one applies to the car.</p>
</div>

<h2>Why Hitting a Deer Is a Comprehensive Claim, Not Collision</h2>

<p>Auto insurance splits physical damage to your vehicle into two coverages. <strong>Collision</strong> pays when your car strikes another vehicle or object — a car, a mailbox, a guardrail. <strong>Comprehensive</strong> (sometimes called "other than collision") pays for events largely outside your control: theft, vandalism, falling trees, hail, fire — and contact with an animal.</p>

<p>Insurers treat animal strikes as comprehensive because a deer bolting into your lane isn't a driving decision — it's closer to a hailstorm than a fender bender. That classification matters for two reasons:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>It's covered even without collision coverage.</strong> Some Michigan drivers carry comprehensive but skip collision on an older vehicle. If that's you, a deer strike is still covered.</li>
  <li><strong>It's generally treated more favorably at renewal.</strong> A comprehensive claim is a not-at-fault claim. It usually affects your premium far less than an at-fault collision claim would — though claim history can still be a rating factor, so ask us before filing a borderline claim.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What If You Swerve and Miss the Deer?</h2>

<p>This is where Michigan drivers get an unwelcome surprise. If you swerve to avoid a deer and hit a tree, a ditch, a fence, or another vehicle — and never touch the deer — the claim is <strong>collision</strong>, because your car struck an object, not an animal. That means:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Your collision deductible applies</strong>, which is often higher than your comprehensive deductible.</li>
  <li><strong>You may be considered at fault</strong> for leaving your lane, which can affect your rates in a way a deer strike would not.</li>
  <li><strong>If you don't carry collision coverage, there's no coverage at all</strong> for your vehicle's damage.</li>
</ul>

<p>That's one more reason safety experts — and every insurance carrier we represent — give the same advice: <strong>don't swerve.</strong> Brake firmly, hold your lane, and let the car's crumple zones do their job. Swerving at speed risks a rollover or a head-on collision that is far more dangerous (and more expensive) than the deer strike itself.</p>

<h2>When Deer Crashes Happen in Michigan</h2>

<p>Michigan's deer herd is estimated at nearly two million animals, and the Michigan State Police report that most vehicle-deer crashes cluster in two windows: <strong>April through June</strong> (spring movement) and <strong>October through December</strong> (breeding season — the worst stretch of the year). Crashes peak at dawn and dusk, and about 80% happen on two-lane roads. Right here in Oakland County — along with the rural roads of Lapeer and Genesee counties — deer crash counts run among the highest in the state, so this is a local problem, not an Up North problem.</p>

<h2>What to Do After Hitting a Deer in Michigan</h2>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Pull over safely and turn on your hazards.</strong> Don't stand in the roadway, and don't approach the deer — an injured deer can be dangerous.</li>
  <li><strong>Call the police.</strong> Michigan law requires you to report a crash involving an injury or property damage of $1,000 or more — and almost any deer strike clears that bar. The police report also documents the claim as an animal strike, which supports the comprehensive classification.</li>
  <li><strong>Photograph everything.</strong> The damage, the scene, and the deer if it's present. Hair, blood, or dents with animal evidence make the comprehensive claim straightforward.</li>
  <li><strong>Don't assume the car is drivable.</strong> Check for leaking fluids, a damaged radiator, or a misaligned hood latch before driving on.</li>
  <li><strong>Call your agent.</strong> We'll confirm your comprehensive deductible, tell you what the claim will look like, and get it filed. If the damage is barely above your deductible, we'll talk honestly about whether filing makes sense.</li>
</ol>

<p>One uniquely Michigan footnote: if the deer is killed, you can generally keep it — ask the responding officer about a free roadkill salvage permit.</p>

<h2>What a Deer Claim Costs — and What It Doesn't</h2>

<p>The average deer strike causes several thousand dollars in damage — grilles, radiators, headlight assemblies, hoods, and the sensors and cameras built into modern front ends add up fast. You'll pay your comprehensive deductible (commonly $250–$500); comprehensive pays the rest of the repair, or the vehicle's actual cash value if it's totaled. If you're financing and the payoff exceeds the car's value, <a href="../michigan-gap-insurance/">gap insurance</a> covers the difference — worth checking before deer season, not after.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Does hitting a deer raise your insurance in Michigan?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Usually not much, if at all. A deer strike is a comprehensive (not-at-fault) claim, and most carriers don't surcharge a single comprehensive claim the way they would an at-fault accident. Multiple comprehensive claims in a short period can still affect your renewal, so if the damage is close to your deductible it may not be worth filing. Call us and we'll run the math with you.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>What if I don't have comprehensive coverage and I hit a deer?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Damage to your vehicle isn't covered — Michigan's required no-fault coverages pay for injuries and for damage you cause to others, not for your own car in an animal strike. Your PIP medical coverage still pays for injuries to you and your passengers. If your vehicle is worth more than a couple thousand dollars, comprehensive coverage is inexpensive protection against Michigan's single most common animal hazard.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Should I swerve to avoid a deer?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>No. Brake hard, stay in your lane, and honk. Swerving risks a rollover or a collision with a tree or oncoming traffic — outcomes that are more dangerous than hitting the deer and that turn a comprehensive claim into a potentially at-fault collision claim.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Does insurance cover deer damage to a parked car?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Yes. If a deer runs into your parked vehicle — it happens more than you'd think — that's still an animal-contact loss under comprehensive coverage, subject to your deductible.</p>
  </div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
  <summary>Do I need a police report to file a deer claim in Michigan?</summary>
  <div class="faq-body">
    <p>Carriers can process a comprehensive claim without one, but you should get one anyway. Michigan law requires reporting crashes with $1,000+ in damage, and the report is the cleanest proof that your claim is an animal strike rather than an at-fault collision. Photos of the scene and the vehicle help too.</p>
  </div>
</details>
